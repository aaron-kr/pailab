# What is Source-Based Research?

Academic research is not just about having ideas — it is about <ins>supporting those ideas with evidence</ins> from reliable sources.

In a nutshell, finding and citing sources involves:

- **Searching:** Find relevant academic information
- **Evaluating:** Decide whether the source is trustworthy
- **Using:** Integrate evidence into your argument
- **Citing:** Give proper credit to original authors

# Core Stages of Finding & Citing Resources

Before anything else, return to your Research Question.<br>
_What information do you need to know to answer your Research Question?_

## 1. Identify Keywords

Example topic:

> “How does <mark>chatbot feedback</mark> affect <mark>student learning outcomes</mark>?”

Possible keywords:

- chatbot feedback
- AI tutoring
- student learning outcomes
- educational technology
- classroom AI

Also try synonyms, broader / narrower terms, or related concepts.

**Search Strategy Example**

- “AI chatbot feedback AND student performance”
- “large language models in education”
- “automated tutoring systems”


## 📋 The Efficient Reading Checklist

Instead of reading from start to finish, check these elements in order to decide if the paper is worth a deep dive:

- **The Abstract:** Does the study's core finding directly relate to your question?
- **The "Gap" Statement:** Usually in the last paragraph of the Introduction. What problem are they solving?
- **The Discussion/Conclusion:** Read this before the Results. It summarizes what the data actually means.
- **The Figures & Tables:** Can you understand the main result just by looking at the charts?
- **The References:** Use the "Breadcrumb" method. Who are they citing frequently? Those are your must-read "seed" papers.

## 🏗️ The Synthesis Matrix (Writing Prep)

Don't just take notes on individual papers. Create a table (Excel or Notion) where each row is a paper and each column is a theme or variable. This allows you to write by "theme" rather than "summary."

| Source (Author, Year) | Research Aim | Methodology | Key Findings | The "Gap" or Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smith (2022) | Remote work productivity | Survey | 15% increase | Only looked at tech sector |
| Doe (2023) | Burnout in tech | Interviews | High correlation | Small sample size |

## 💡 Pro-Tips for Speed

- **"Search & Destroy":** Use <kbd>Cmd+F</kbd> (or <kbd>Ctrl+F</kbd>) for keywords like <mark>"however"</mark>, <mark>"limitations"</mark>, <mark>"future research"</mark>, or <mark>"significant."</mark> 
- **AI Summarizers:** Use tools like [Moonlight](https://www.themoonlight.io/), [Scholarcy](https://www.scholarcy.com/) or [Consensus](https://consensus.app/) to get a high-level "flashcard" of the paper's main claims before you open the PDF.<br>(More on using AI in [Unit 9 →](./unit-09-using-ai-in-research).)
- **The 10-Minute Rule:** If you haven't found a reason to cite the paper within 10 minutes, stop reading and move to the next one.

# Step 4: References

Most conferences and journals we publish in use **IEEE style** for references. This means:
- References are numbered in the order they appear in the text (e.g., [1], [2], etc.)
- The reference list at the end of the paper is ordered by these numbers, not alphabetically
- Each reference includes specific information such as author names, paper title, conference/journal name, year, etc.
- For example:
   - [1] J. Doe and A. Smith, "Title of the Paper," in *Proceedings of the Conference Name*, Year, pp. 1-10.
   - [2] J. Doe, "Title of the Journal Article," *Journal Name*, vol. 10, no. 2, pp. 100-110, Year.

Try to include:
- 3–6 references for a conference paper
- 10+ references for a journal paper

Main idea: Good research depends on trustworthy sources and clear citation practices.

What is Source-Based Research?
Academic research is not just about having ideas — it is about supporting those ideas with evidence from reliable sources.
In a nutshell, finding and citing sources involves:
Searching: Find relevant academic information
Evaluating: Decide whether the source is trustworthy
Using: Integrate evidence into your argument
Citing: Give proper credit to original authors
This process can be visualized as a cycle:
Question → Search → Evaluate → Read → Use Evidence → Cite → Revise → (back to Search)

3. Evaluate Source Credibility
Not all sources are equally reliable.
A good source is usually:
Written by experts
Published by reputable journals or institutions
Supported by evidence
Recent enough for the topic
Relevant to your research question
The CRAAP Test
A common method for evaluating sources:
C — Currency
Is the information recent enough?
R — Relevance
Does it directly relate to your topic?
A — Authority
Who is the author or organization?
A — Accuracy
Is the evidence supported and verifiable?
P — Purpose
Is the source informative, persuasive, biased, or commercial?

4. Read Sources Efficiently
You do NOT need to read every paper word-for-word.
A fast reading strategy:
Read the title
Read the abstract
Read the introduction
Read the conclusion
Scan figures/tables
Only then read details if necessary
Key goal:
Understand the paper’s question, method, and findings.

5. Take Organized Notes
Good note-taking prevents confusion later.
Track:
Main argument
Important evidence
Useful quotations
Page numbers
Citation information
Your own thoughts/questions
A simple note format:
Source
Key Finding
Useful Quote
Your Notes


6. Use Sources Ethically
Sources should support your ideas — not replace them.
Avoid:
❌ Copying text without citation
 ❌ Paraphrasing too closely
 ❌ Using unreliable websites as evidence
Instead:
✔ Summarize findings in your own words
 ✔ Compare multiple sources
 ✔ Explain WHY the evidence matters

7. Understand Citations

They help readers:

8. In-Text Citation Basics
APA Example
Paraphrase:
AI tutoring systems may improve engagement in online learning (Smith, 2024).
Direct quote:
“Students responded more positively to immediate chatbot feedback” (Smith, 2024, p. 18).

9. Build a Reference List
Every in-text citation should match a full reference.
APA Journal Example
Smith, J. (2024). AI tutoring systems and student engagement. Journal of Educational Technology, 12(3), 44–59.
Key point:
Citation consistency matters more than memorizing every rule.

10. Use Citation Tools Carefully
Helpful tools include:
Zotero
Mendeley
EndNote
Citation generators
These tools save time, but:
⚠ Always double-check formatting errors.

11. Avoid Plagiarism
Plagiarism means presenting someone else’s work as your own.
This includes:
Copying without quotation marks
Missing citations
Improper paraphrasing
Reusing your own previous work without disclosure
Good practice:
When in doubt, cite the source.

12. Synthesize Sources
Strong research combines multiple sources into one discussion.
Weak writing:
❌ “Source A says this. Source B says this.”
Better writing:
✔ “While Source A suggests chatbot feedback improves engagement, Source B argues the effect depends on feedback timing.”
Key point:
Research writing is a conversation between sources.

Key Concepts
Primary vs Secondary Sources
Primary Sources
Original evidence or first-hand research
Examples:
Experiments
Surveys
Interviews
Original datasets
Secondary Sources
Analysis or discussion of primary research
Examples:
Review articles
Textbooks
Commentary papers

Peer Review
Peer-reviewed research is evaluated by experts before publication.
This generally improves reliability and academic quality.

DOI (Digital Object Identifier)
A DOI is a permanent identifier for academic articles.
Example:
doi:10.xxxx/xxxxx
Useful because links may change, but DOIs usually remain stable.

Using only Google searches

Forgetting citation information

Think of Sources As:
“Evidence supporting an argument.”
Not:
❌ random information
 ❌ filler content
 ❌ copied summaries

Checklist

 ☐ Did you organize notes clearly?
