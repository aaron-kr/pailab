# What is Academic Research?

> Most research starts unclear and becomes clearer through iteration.

In a nutshell, academic research is an iterative process of:

1. Asking clear, structured _questions_
2. Systematically _investigating_ those questions 
   - through _experiments_ 
   - and _data analysis_
3. _Communicating your findings_ in a clear and compelling way

This process can be visualized as a cycle:

1. **Question** → 2. **Experiment** → 3. **Data Analysis** → 4. **References** → 5. **Writing** → 6. **Revision** → (back to 1)

# Core Stages of the Research Process

1. **Question**: What problem are you trying to solve? Write it in one sentence.
2. **Experiment**: Design and conduct experiments to investigate your question.
3. **Data Analysis**: Analyze the data you collect _from your experiments_ to draw conclusions.
4. **References**: Find and cite relevant previous work to situate your research in the existing landscape.
5. **Writing**: Communicate your research clearly and effectively.
6. **Revision**: Refine your paper based on feedback and new insights.

# Choose Your Contribution

Your paper should do one of these:
- Test an idea
- Apply an idea in a new context
- Propose a new approach

3. Core Stages of the Research Process
1. Choosing a Topic

You start by identifying a general area of interest.

Good topics are:

Specific enough to investigate
Open to unanswered questions
Feasible within time/resources

Bad topics are:

Too broad (“AI in society”)
Too narrow or already fully solved
2. Forming a Research Question

A research question is the center of the entire project.

A good question is:

Clear
Focused
Answerable
Measurable or arguable

Examples:

❌ “What is AI?”
✔ “How does chatbot feedback affect student learning outcomes?”
3. Background Research (Literature Review)

You study what others have already discovered.

Goals:

Avoid repeating existing work
Understand key concepts
Identify gaps or open problems

Key skill: reading papers efficiently, not deeply at first.

4. Designing the Method

You decide how you will answer your question.

This may involve:

Experiments
Surveys
Simulations
Case studies
Data analysis

Important concept:

Your method determines what kind of answer you can get.

5. Data Collection / Investigation

You gather evidence.

Examples:

Experimental results
Survey responses
Observations
Datasets

Key principles:

Be consistent
Be transparent
Avoid bias
6. Analysis

You interpret the data using logic, statistics, or reasoning.

Questions to ask:

What patterns do I see?
What does the data actually show?
Are there alternative explanations?
7. Conclusion & Interpretation

You connect results back to your original question.

Good conclusions:

Answer the research question directly
Acknowledge limitations
Avoid overclaiming
8. Communication

Research is not complete until it is communicated.

Forms include:

Research papers
Posters
Presentations
Reports

Core idea:

If no one understands it, it doesn’t function as research.

4. Key Concepts to Remember
Hypothesis vs Question
Question: what you want to know
Hypothesis: your predicted answer
Correlation vs Causation
Correlation = two things move together
Causation = one directly affects the other
Validity

Are you measuring what you think you are measuring?

Reliability

Would you get the same result if repeated?

Reproducibility

Can someone else repeat your process and get similar results?

5. Common Beginner Mistakes
Choosing overly broad topics
Skipping literature review
Changing the question too late
Collecting data without a clear method
Jumping to conclusions too quickly
Ignoring negative results
6. Mental Model of Research

Think of research as:

“A structured argument supported by evidence.”

Not:

guessing
opinion writing
summarizing information
